# Order-Flow and Auction-Structure Feature Research Plan for ES Intraday

*Reference: strategy/research only. Current operator interface is CLI-only; see [../README.md](../README.md) and [../OPERATOR.md](../OPERATOR.md).*

## 1. Data Sources and Quality  
- **Tick/BBO/Depth data providers:** High-fidelity tick and depth data are essential.  Major providers include Databento (CME, ICE, etc., full order book and trades)【4†L41-L49】, CQG (historical tick and best-bid/offer feeds)【4†L41-L49】, Rithmic (ultra-low-latency trades and quotes), dxFeed (real-time and historical tick and full depth), TickData/OneMarketData (clean historical tick depth archives)【4†L75-L83】, and IQFeed.  These can supply the ES trades and top-of-book/depth needed for order-flow studies.  Licensed exchange feeds or data-warehouse solutions (e.g. CME DataMine’s Market Depth files) are also available.  
- **Data quality:** Ensure a high “order flow data quality” – i.e. complete and timely ticks and depth updates【26†L339-L347】.  In fast markets, slow or incomplete feeds can make pressure look artificially strong and hide “absorption”【26†L339-L347】.  Any missing ticks, delayed book updates or mis-sorted trades will distort signals.  Institutional-grade feeds (with all trades and frequent depth updates) are recommended【26†L339-L347】.  Before testing, validate that volume and liquidity changes in the data behave logically with price moves (e.g. volume spikes with volatility)【26†L290-L298】【26†L339-L347】.  

## 2. Key Order-Flow Features  
For each feature family below, we define its essence, data requirements, typical use-cases, and pitfalls.  

- **Cumulative Volume Delta (CVD):** Tracks net buying vs. selling volume.  Concretely, CVD is computed as the running sum of *(volume at the ask – volume at the bid)* (or equivalently *buying volume minus selling volume*) over time【7†L33-L40】【7†L60-L68】.  Rising CVD means more aggressive buying (bullish pressure) and falling means selling pressure【7†L33-L40】.  **Data needed:** Trade-by-trade data with aggressor side (or reconstructed via tick streams identifying trades at the bid vs ask price).  **Useful for:** Gauging underlying sentiment in a trend or flagging divergences (price rising while CVD falls can signal waning buyer interest)【7†L33-L40】.  It is often used as a **trend filter** or *confirmation* signal.  **Pitfalls:** Requires accurate labeling of trades at bid/ask; reconstructed CVD from aggregated bars (e.g. using up/down volume estimates) loses fidelity.  Large spikes (e.g. block trades) can dominate CVD, so outlier filtering or capped increments may be needed.  Also, purely following CVD without price structure often yields false signals; e.g. a big buy spike might just exhaust at resistance.  

- **Order-Book Imbalance / BBO Pressure:** Measures the difference between bid-side and ask-side liquidity or executed volume.  For example, one computes an *imbalance ratio* = (bid-volume – ask-volume) / (bid-volume + ask-volume) at the best price(s) or over a short time window.  In practice, a heavily skewed book (e.g. far more bids than asks at top levels) suggests buying pressure; the converse suggests selling pressure【9†L41-L47】【14†L95-L104】.  **Data needed:** Real-time level-2 order book (depth data) for limit volumes, and/or time-and-sales to measure executed volume on each side.  **Use-cases:** Spotting imminent breaks or reversals: a large imbalance on one side can precede a short-term move (e.g. a big bid-wall suggests a temporary support zone)【14†L95-L104】.  It can serve as a **short-horizon directional signal**【28†L47-L52】 or an entry trigger when combined with price patterns.  **Pitfalls:** Book imbalance is extremely sensitive to spoofing/manipulation【9†L91-L99】.  Fake large orders create artificial imbalance【9†L91-L99】.  Also, high-frequency cancellations (“flickering quotes”) can make snapshots misleading – always confirm with tape or duration of liquidity.  The imbalance metric must be normalized (e.g. by total depth) to avoid biases in varying liquidity.  Beware interpreting a static imbalance as trend proof; it may only be transient.  

- **Market Order Aggression:** Quantifies the aggressiveness of executed trades.  A common heuristic is: **prints at the ask (or above)** are *aggressive buys*, **prints at the bid (or below)** are *aggressive sells*【31†L182-L189】.  Key measures include the rate and size of prints at bid/ask over short intervals.  **Data:** Tick-level time-and-sales (timestamp, price, size) with best-bid/ask context.  **Use-cases:** Entry triggers and exhaustion signals.  For example, a surge of large prints hitting the ask (aggressive buys) could signal an upward breakout; if price moves little despite heavy aggressor buys, it indicates **absorption** by sellers【31†L189-L197】.  Conversely, prints at bid with price holding suggests hidden demand.  **Pitfalls:** A few big prints can be noise; volume must be contextualized (compare to recent average, see *Volume Spike* below).  Over-reliance on aggressive prints can misfire if prints cluster at edges of a range.  As with imbalance, spoof prints (rapid small fills) can mislead.  Always confirm aggression with sustained impact or multiple ticks.  

- **Volume Spike / Infusion:** Detects abnormally high volume in a bar relative to history.  For instance, flag a bar if its total volume exceeds \(N\)-bar moving average by a factor (e.g. >2×)【14†L117-L125】.  **Data:** Bar-level volume or tick aggregation.  **Use-cases:** Spot institutional entries or volatility jumps (often preluding breakouts).  It is used as a **volatility or momentum filter**: e.g. require a volume spike to confirm a breakout signal.  **Pitfalls:** Market-wide volume events (news) can trigger spikes everywhere; by itself it lacks direction.  The threshold needs tuning; too low and nearly every bar spikes, too high and you miss signals.  Watch for *log-scale* or adaptive threshold so not dominated by outliers.  

- **Absorption / Exhaustion:** Observes when aggressive order flow fails to move price.  **Definition:** If large market buy orders hit the ask but price stalls or reverses, sellers are absorbing demand【31†L191-L199】.  Vice versa for selling.  **Data:** Time-and-sales plus live price.  **Use-cases:** Used as a **reversal or exit signal**.  For example, if an uptrend’s peak is met with absorption (many buys, no rally), one might exit longs or even short.  It can also fine-tune entries (avoid buying into absorption).  **Pitfalls:** Requires quick reaction – often only visible *after* many ticks.  Data latency or missing prints can mask absorption.  It’s also noise-prone: many small prints can mimic slow absorption.  To operationalize, set a volume threshold *and* price stasis condition (e.g. >X contracts with <Y tick move).  Beware equating any high-volume bar with true absorption: volume can run high yet price follow through.  

- **Volume Profile / Value Area / POC:** These chart the distribution of volume across prices within a time window.  The **Point of Control (POC)** is the price with highest traded volume; the **value area** often the range covering ~70% of volume.  **Data:** Aggregated trade ticks (price & quantity).  **Use-cases:** Identify high-interest price zones as support/resistance.  For example, if the market is approaching a POC or upper edge of a value area, it may slow.  Useful as context (“fair value” region) and to set entries/exits (fade edges, trade moves to re-enter value).  Often recomputed for each trading window.  **Pitfalls:** Static volume profiles lag price moves – they tell where participants *have* traded, not what they will do next.  Using the wrong period leaks future info (lookahead) – only use completed bars to build the profile.  Also, low-volume spikes (e.g. one block trade) can distort POC; filtering or percentile VP (excluding top outliers) may help.  Don’t treat volume-profile levels as infallible – price often ignores prior profile if new supply/demand emerges.  

- **Anchored VWAP:** A volume-weighted average price calculated from a specific start time or event, not the session open【21†L357-L364】.  For example, anchor VWAP at yesterday’s close or at a known pivot (low/high)【21†L357-L364】.  **Data:** Trade ticks (price × volume) and timestamps.  **Use-cases:** Provides a dynamic “fair price” reference since the anchor event.  Trading above the AVWAP implies buyers have been dominant since that point, below implies sellers lead【21†L357-L364】.  It’s used for **trade planning**: e.g. if price pulls back toward the AVWAP after a breakout, it can be a low-risk entry, or watching for rejection at AVWAP as a reversal signal.  **Pitfalls:** Anchor must be defined based on past events known at run-time.  Anchoring on a local extreme or breakout candle *after* it occurs must be handled carefully in testing (don’t include future ticks).  AVWAP can also “whipsaw” in choppy markets – it’s not inherently predictive, so should be combined with structure (e.g. only use AVWAP bounces in clear trends)【21†L333-L342】【21†L348-L354】.  Note too that anchored VWAP relies on volume; thin volumes (overnight, holiday) make it noisy.  

## 3. Bar-based vs. Tick-based Features  
Features that fundamentally rely on sequence or side of trades **require tick/BBO data**.  In particular, CVD, true order-book imbalance, aggression and absorption need trade-by-trade data with price levels.  As explained, compressing to OHLC bars *loses* information about trade timing, price distribution, and bid/ask fill ratio【24†L102-L110】.  For example, knowing bar‐close price and total volume does **not** reveal how much was buying vs selling, or where volume clustered.  Only tick data preserves that detail【24†L102-L110】.  

By contrast, some features can be approximated from bars if only aggregate stats are available: e.g. a coarse “delta” could be approximated by (up-bar volume – down-bar volume) if bar colors are defined by close vs open, but this is crude.  VWAP (including anchored VWAP) can be computed from bar prices and volume if one treats each bar’s VWAP*volume as an aggregate (though tick precision is lost).  Volume profile can be built if intrabar volume-by-price info exists; if not, one could approximate a rough profile from an array of bar high/low/close and volume, but it’s very imprecise.  In general, **any feature that uses bid/ask side or intrabar price distribution truly needs tick/BBO data**.  

## 4. Experimental Designs  

- **Directional Signal Tests:** For each feature (or combination), test if it predicts short-term price movement.  E.g., compute the feature on bars or rolling windows and regress it against future returns.  Measure hit-rate or correlation – e.g. does a positive CVD or strong buy imbalance reliably precede a price uptick?  Use cross-validation: fit signals on one period, test on out-of-sample.  (Arbitrary statistical tests like information coefficient can rank features.)  Ensure no lookahead: a feature should only use data up to time *t* when predicting move after *t*.  For example, one could label 1-minute returns and test if high aggression on a bar statistically leads to mean price change.  The cited literature finds *order-book imbalance* often has a short-horizon predictive edge【28†L47-L52】.  

- **Filter / Contextual Use:** Evaluate order-flow features as trade filters.  For example, implement your baseline intraday strategy (with VWAP, ATR, MA slope, etc.) and then only allow trades when an order-flow condition holds (e.g. CVD > 0 for buys).  Compare performance vs. unfiltered.  Alternatively, measure performance *without* order-flow and then *with* (keeping other rules constant).  The benefit (or loss) in metrics (Sharpe, P&L, hit rate) quantifies filtering value.  (This is akin to “logic bump testing” where one condition is added/removed.)  Use a variety of threshold values for robustness.  

- **Execution Trigger / Timing:** Some order-flow signals may improve entry/exit timing.  For example, simulate the strategy entering a position only when a trade executes through the NBBO (aggressive order) or when the best bid/ask is lifted.  Compare slippage vs. using passive entry.  One could backtest the difference between “limit-at-touch” versus “market” orders signaled by order-flow cues.  Similarly, test exit triggers: e.g. exit long when absorption is detected.  This may require a simulator that tracks partial fills and queue positions.  

- **Exit Signal:** Test if order-flow cues help exits.  For example, implement a rule: “If price is up but shows strong sell imbalance (aggressive sells + large ask wall), then exit long.”  Compare exit timing and trailing returns with and without this rule.  Alternatively, measure how often an exit signal (like a drop in CVD) coincides with adverse price moves (justifying its use).  

- **Confidence / Position Sizing:** Order-flow metrics can modulate position size or leverage.  Test this by adjusting trade size by the feature magnitude: e.g. double size if CVD is strongly positive, half if weak.  Backtest if this scaling improves risk-adjusted returns.  (One could treat order-flow value as a “signal strength” used in a probabilistic model.)  A/B test: fixed size vs. flow-weighted size.  

In all experiments, use **controlled conditions**: keep other parameters fixed when isolating a feature, and run across multiple market regimes.  Use walk-forward validation to avoid overfitting.  Ablation studies (removing each feature from a multi-factor model) can show incremental value beyond VWAP/ATR/MA etc.

## 5. Feature Value Ranking (Hypothetical)  
Based on literature and practical usage, we might expect roughly:  
1. **Order-Book Imbalance / Pressure:** Often cited as a direct microstructure signal (e.g. Cont *et al.*【28†L47-L52】). Quick to respond to supply/demand shifts.  
2. **Cumulative Volume Delta:** A broad sentiment filter; valuable for context/trend confirmation, though not very sharp on its own.  
3. **Anchored VWAP:** Useful context for entries/exits around major moves (especially if anchored at session open or breaks), though not a fast signal.  
4. **Market Aggression / Absorption:** Very high-frequency in nature – can provide immediate trade triggers in active periods (good for scalping). But also noisy.  
5. **Volume Profile / POC:** More structural; provides key levels but less predictive. Good for marking zones.  
6. **Volume Spikes (Infusion):** Helpful as confirmation (e.g. only take breakouts on spikes), but low standalone value.  

This ranking is subjective: in practice, relative value depends on horizon and execution style (scalpers may rank Aggression/Absorption higher; swing traders rely more on VWAP, volume profile).  

## 6. Time-of-Day (“Hot Zone”) Suitability  
The given windows have different liquidity and volatility profiles:  

- **6:30–8:30 (Pre-open/Morning Globex):** Generally lower volume (ahead of US cash open).  Order-flow signals here can be erratic (low depth, high bid-ask noise).  *Use sparingly:* mostly, this period is good for context (e.g. note overnight accumulation levels via volume profile), but real-time cues are less reliable.  Avoid treating small imbalances as directional.  

- **9:00–11:00 (US Open to Mid-Morning):** High activity.  Order-flow features shine here.  **Imbalances** and **aggression** can capture open interest surges.  For example, at 9:30, large institutional flows hit and a big buy-delta or trade prints may predict the day’s initial move.  **Volume spikes** and breakouts with real order-flow confirmation are useful filters.  Absorption at key levels around this time often signals early reversals (test with LOD/HOD).  Order-flow filters can add value against the volatility of open spikes.  

- **12:00–13:00 (Lunch Period):** Thinner and choppier.  Use order-flow mostly as a **context indicator**.  For instance, if a level of interest is known (POC from morning), watch if mid-day trades are respecting it.  Aggressive orders mean more here (less total flow) – so only very significant prints should move price.  Anchored VWAP from open/previous day may guide range boundaries.  

- **12:45–13:00 (Overlap of US session midday, etc.):** This short nested window might coincide with European close or options events – volume can pick up.  Order-flow signals can be sharp but brief.  For example, a sudden imbalance at 12:50 could precede the 1:00 rollback in some strategies.  Use it as a final check before the system’s usual exit.  

In summary, **busy periods (open, major economic news)** favor fast order-flow signals (imbalance, aggression). **Quieter periods** favor accumulative or structural features (VWAP, profile) for filtering out noise.

## 7. Research Stack (Tools & Data)  
- **Minimal Stack:** An intraday backtester (in Python, R, or similar) that can handle tick-level or minute-level data.  For example, Python with pandas and numpy for data manipulation, TA-lib for technicals, and vectorbt/backtrader/zipline for backtesting.  Acquire historical ES data: at least 1- or 5-second bars with bid/ask (Level 1) or time-and-sales.  Even a 1-minute bar with (Open,High,Low,Close,Volume) plus a separate cumulative delta estimate can test some features, but true limit-side info needs tick data.  For data, one could start with a source like Databento or Norgate for historical futures; or even broker-provided (Tradovate, Rithmic) for tick data.【4†L41-L49】  
- **Advanced Stack:** A time-series database or tick database (kdb+, TickSQL, Arctic) to store years of tick/BBO data. Event-driven simulation framework (e.g. custom C++/Python engine) to replay the order book. GPU or parallel processing to compute heavy features (e.g. all-level imbalance). Real-time streaming and low-latency links for forward-testing live. Tools like Bookmap or Sierra Chart can be used for visualization but are optional. In a fully quantitative setup, one might integrate machine learning toolkits (scikit-learn, XGBoost) to test non-linear combinations of features. High-performance data handling (e.g. using Numba or Cython) may be needed for millions of ticks.  

## 8. Incremental Value Tests (vs. Baselines)  
To ensure order-flow features add beyond basic signals, perform **ablation and incremental tests**:  
- **Baseline model:** Use standard features – intraday VWAP deviations, ATR (volatility measure), moving-average slopes, and simple range-breakout flags.  This is your control.  
- **Add one feature at a time:** For each order-flow family, augment the model or strategy and measure improvement.  For example, add an “imbalance filter” that requires buy imbalance for long entries.  Compare key metrics (return/risk, hit-rate) against baseline.  A significant lift indicates incremental value.  
- **Statistical tests:** If using a predictive model, check if order-flow features have non-zero regression coefficients when controlling for VWAP/ATR etc.  Use information ratio or Sharpe increase.  Perform forward-testing or cross-validation to avoid overfit.  
- **Cross-correlation analysis:** Some order-flow metrics may be correlated with baseline (e.g. high ATR often accompanies volume spikes).  Compute partial correlations to ensure unique contribution.  

This approach quantifies how much extra edge each feature provides.  If an order-flow feature has near-zero incremental gain, it may be redundant.  Conversely, a large lift means a strong predictor beyond common indicators.

## 9. Risks: Lookahead, Overfitting, and Data Issues  
- **Lookahead bias:** Many order-flow metrics depend on events (e.g. anchoring after a breakout candle). In backtests, ensure the anchor is set using only information up to that time.  Similarly, computing “value area” typically uses full-window volume – in live trading only past bars are known, so simulate it incrementally.  
- **Parameter tuning / data-snooping:** Order-flow features often have thresholds (e.g. imbalance ratio, volume spike multiplier). Avoid optimizing these on full history. Use walk-forward and multiple periods to choose robust values.  
- **Data lags and mismatches:** If backtesting on consolidated feeds but live trading uses a different feed, you may see degraded signals. For example, if your tick dataset excludes certain exchange fills or routes, your CVD will misstate pressure.  Regularly compare live vs. historical data quality.  
- **Spurious correlations:** In thin sessions, random trades can create apparent imbalance. Always test features in multiple liquidity regimes.  
- **Execution slippage:** If using market order triggers from order-flow, account for slippage in backtest (markets may move between signal and fill).  Overlooking execution cost can falsely inflate the value of fast signals.  

## 10. Final Recommendations  

- **High-priority features:** Start with **order-book imbalance at best prices** and **cumulative volume delta**.  These have the clearest economic interpretation (supply/demand pressure) and historical precedent as predictors【28†L47-L52】【7†L33-L40】.  They can serve immediately as context filters (e.g. require bullish delta to confirm a long entry).  Next, implement **anchored VWAP** from session starts or known pivots (using only past data) to guide trades around “fair value”.  It’s simple to code and provides a stabilizing baseline.  

- **Moderate-value features:** Add **market order aggression/absorption** measures for execution timing.  For example, monitor how many large prints hit bid/ask in recent seconds (as in [31†L182-L189]).  Use this primarily to adjust timing (e.g. delay an entry if aggressive buying meets resistance).  Volume spikes (infusions) can be added later as a confirmation layer (only trade breakouts when volume >2× normal) once the basic system is stable.

- **Low-priority / avoid initially:** Complex *auction-specific* metrics (e.g. dynamically shifting value-area POCs, multi-anchor VWAP combinations) are interesting but risk overfitting.  Similarly, fine-grained depth imbalance beyond top levels is alluring but often just noise for a system targeting minute-scale moves.  Avoid relying on any hand-wavy “read tape” rule without clear definition.

- **Role of order flow (Alpha vs. Filter vs. Execution):** Don’t expect order-flow features to be *standalone* alpha generators in a longer-term intraday strategy.  They are most reliable as *filters or execution aids*.  For instance, an ES breakout signal is stronger if accompanied by positive volume delta or a cleared ask wall; a mean-reversion exit is more credible if absorption is seen.  In other words, use order-flow to **weight confidence** in signals. As an execution support, order-flow can fine-tune entries (e.g. add a slight delay until an aggressive print confirms direction) and exits (e.g. take profit if absorption is detected ahead of target).  

By following this systematic plan, you can rigorously test which order-flow and auction features truly enhance your ES intraday model, while guarding against lookahead or data pitfalls.
